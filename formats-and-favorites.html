Formats and Favorites

Mon 04 March 2019
By Joshuah Kapilivsky

Tennis doesn't have a history of making it easy to follow the score. It's not like soccer, basketball, or baseball, where you just see who scores the most goals, points, or runs. In tennis, you win matches by winning sets, you win sets by winning games, and you win games by winning points. This hierarchical scoring system is very interesting from a probabilistic perspective.

Though it is quite complicated, most people can keep up with the way tennis is scored. However, you'd have to be a pretty serious fan of tennis to keep up with the different tennis scoring systems, even if you stick to the biggest events of the year.

At Slams, the WTA plays best of 3 sets and the ATP plays best of 5 sets. Certain Slams used to play the final set to "Advantage", meaning that players could not win in the deciding set unless they won by two games. Recently, some of the Slams have decided to include tiebreaks in the final set, though with variations:

  • Australian Open - Final set tiebreak at 6-6, played to 10, win by 2
  • French Open - Final set first to 6 games, win by 2
  • Wimbledon - Final set tiebreak at 12-12, played to 7, win by 2
  • US Open - Final set tiebreak at 6-6, played to 7, win by 2

That's four Slams, with four different scoring systems. That makes eight scoring systems when you consider the number of sets played by the WTA and the ATP. And I'm only talking about singles; doubles has different scoring systems across the Slams, which I won't go into here.

Then we can consider a relatively new event: the Next Generation ATP Finals, an annual tournament for the sport's best men aged 21 and under. Those matches are best of 5 sets, first to 4 games, with no-ad scoring and tiebreaks at 3-3 (more details here).

All of these variations open up even more research questions. Here I begin to untangle the probabilistic intricacies of these tennis formats.

Part 1: Tennis as (Biased) Coin Flips¶

I'm going to quantify the differences in tennis formats using a basic model of a tennis match as a series of (biased) coin flips. I'm going to use this model to determine the probability of winning the match given one input value for each player: the expected percent of points won on serve.

This model of tennis is often referred to as the Independent and Identically Distributed model (IID). I think it is most easily understood as a simulation. Imagine doing the following exercise:

Go out to a tennis court and play a full match of tennis, but every time you would play a point, flip a coin instead. If it comes up heads, you win the point. Keep score as normal.

While this may not be as fun as playing tennis, it is still a useful mental exercise. If tennis is just a series of coin flips with complicated scoring rules, we can model the process using basic ideas of probability.

Of course, there are (at least) two glaringly obvious flaws with this model:

  1. Tennis is asymmetric, particularly because of the serve. (Serving is an advantage for non-novices).
  2. Some players are better at serving than others.

We can remedy issue one by introducing a biased coin into our exercise. If servers win 70% of points on their serve, then we'll use a coin that comes up heads 70% of the time, and we can take turns flipping it depending on who is "serving".

The remedy for issue two is similar: instead of passing the coin back and forth, we can each have our own biased coin, which comes up heads just as often as we can be expected to win points on each of our serves.

That's the whole idea of the model: you get a coin tuned to your service ability, and I get one tuned to my service ability. Then we take turns flipping our coins until someone wins. We're flipping coins, so the individual point outcomes are independent of each other, and they are identically distributed.

Limitations of Tennis as (Biased) Coin Flips¶

Here's a basic list of things that this model does not consider:

  • Return ability
  • Court surface
  • Play style
  • Player matchups (i.e., something about the pair of us makes me perform better against you than I do against similarly skilled players)
  • Correlation between outcomes/streakiness/momentum
  • "Clutch" Performance Tendencies

This model basically tells us that if you tend to win more points on your serve than your opponent does, the odds are good that you will win the match.

So Why Even Bother?¶

Consider the following matchup:

  1. You tend to win 70% of your service points.
  2. I tend to win 65% of my service points.

Who's going to win the match?

It's obvious without this model that you are more likely to win than I am. Without any calculations, I expect the following things to be true:

  1. The player that tends to win more of their service points is more likely to win the match.
  2. Increasing your rate of service/return points won always increases your probability of winning the match.
  3. A longer match format always improves the probability of the player that tends to win more of their service points winning the match.

Beyond that, it's hard to make any specific conclusions without doing some analysis. Here's a short list of things that you might still want to know about:

  1. What is the probability of a player who wins X% of service points winning against a player who wins Y% of service points?
  2. What is the most likely score for a single set between these two players? How likely is each possible score for the set?
  3. Does who serves first affect each player's probability of winning? Does it affect the probability of obtaining a certain score for the set?
  4. By how much does improving your rate of service/return points won increase your probability of winning the match?
  5. By how much does playing a longer match improve the probability of the "better" player winning?
  6. What is the effect of the tiebreak? The final set tiebreak? Ad scoring?

So now we're back to the original question: what is the effect of different tennis formats on favorites?

Or less precisely:

If Serena and Federer are competing for the most Slam titles, how much of a statistical advantage does Federer get from playing best of 5 sets, instead of best of 3?

If I'm Stefanos Tsitsipas, would I rather play against Alex de Minaur at a Slam or at the Next Generation ATP Finals? What's the ratio of prize money required to make the matches equally attractive?

If I'm running a tennis tournament, how short can I make a tennis match while preserving the advantage of the world's best players? Or how can I make upsets more likely to make matches more dramatic?

Winning a game, set, or match at a particular score has a lot in common with the binomial distribution, which gives us the probability of success on n trials, if the probability of success on each trial is p. A game of tennis is simply first to four points, win by two. (In no-ad scoring it is simply first to four points). So the probability of winning a game at love is simply:

P(Win at love) = binomial(4 successes, 4 trials, p(win point))

However, the probability of winning a game four points to one is not obtained from the binomial distribution, with four successes in five trials. This is because one way to get four successes in five trials is to succeed four straight times and fail on the fifth trial. But in a game of tennis, you wouldn't play the fifth point if you'd already won.

Fortunately, tennis fans are already familiar with a conceptual framework that makes this calculation simple: game points. The probability of winning four points to one is equal to the probability of being up 40-15 (three points to one) and winning that point. The probability of having game point, up 40-15, can be obtained from the binomial distribution with three successes on four trials.

More generally, I use the following approach to calculating the probability of winning with a certain score:

P(Win at X) = P(Winning Position) * P(Win)

Each of the following four code cells defines a function for the probability of winning a game, tiebreak, set, and match.

def p_game(p_point, ad_scoring):
    # probability of winning at certain score is product of probability of winning a point
    # and the probability of getting game point at the necessary score
    
    # holding at love
    # P(40-0) * P(win point)
    w_love = stats.binom.pmf(3, 3, p_point) * p_point
    l_love = stats.binom.pmf(0, 3, p_point) * (1 - p_point)
    
    # holding at 15
    # P(40-15) * P(win point)
    w_15 = stats.binom.pmf(3, 4, p_point) * p_point
    l_15 = stats.binom.pmf(1, 4, p_point) * (1 - p_point)
    
    # holding at 30
    w_30 = stats.binom.pmf(3, 5, p_point) * p_point
    l_30 = stats.binom.pmf(2, 5, p_point) * (1 - p_point)
    
    # getting to deuce
    p_d = stats.binom.pmf(3, 6, p_point)
    
    if ad_scoring:
        # WINNING AT DEUCE:
        # to win after getting to deuce, you have to win two points in a row
        # OR get to another deuce and win two points in a row
        # once at deuce, probability of getting to the next deuce is:
        # 2 * p_point * (1 - p_point)
        # probability of getting to n more deuces (2 * p_point * (1 - p_point)) ** n
        # probability of winning at deuce is the sum of an inifinite geometric series
        # Sum Inf Geom Series(a1, r) = a1 / (1 - r)
        w_d = p_d * p_point ** 2 / (1 - 2 * p_point * (1 - p_point))
        l_d = p_d * (1- p_point) ** 2 / (1 - 2 * p_point * (1 - p_point))
    else:
        w_d = p_d * p_point
        l_d = p_d * p_point
    
    p_win = w_love + w_15 + w_30 + w_d
    probabilities = [w_love, w_15, w_30, w_d, l_love, l_15, l_30, l_d]
    return p_win

def p_tiebreak(p_point_a, p_point_b, tiebreak_to):
    p_win = 0
    
    # win by more than 2
    for n_points in range(tiebreak_to, 2 * tiebreak_to - 1):
        losing_score = n_points - tiebreak_to
        p_winning_position = 0
        p_losing_position = 0
        
        n_service_points = math.floor(n_points / 2)
        n_return_points = math.floor((n_points - 1) / 2)
        
        for paired_mini_breaks in range(losing_score + 1):
            n_holds_winning = n_service_points - paired_mini_breaks
            n_mini_breaks_winning = (tiebreak_to - 1) - n_holds_winning
            p_winning_position += (
                  stats.binom.pmf(n_holds_winning, n_service_points, p_point_a)
                * stats.binom.pmf(n_mini_breaks_winning, n_return_points, 1 - p_point_b))
            
            n_holds_losing = losing_score - paired_mini_breaks
            n_mini_breaks_losing = paired_mini_breaks
            p_losing_position += (
                  stats.binom.pmf(n_holds_losing, n_service_points, p_point_a)
                * stats.binom.pmf(n_mini_breaks_losing, n_return_points, 1 - p_point_b))
            
        if n_points % 2:
            p_win += p_winning_position * p_point_a
        else:
            p_win += p_winning_position * (1 - p_point_b)

    #extended tiebreak
    p_extended = 0
    for paired_mini_breaks in range(tiebreak_to):
        n_service_points = tiebreak_to - 1
        n_return_points = n_service_points
        p_extended += (
                  stats.binom.pmf(n_service_points - paired_mini_breaks,
                                  n_service_points,
                                  p_point_a)
                * stats.binom.pmf(paired_mini_breaks,
                                  n_return_points,
                                  1 - p_point_b))

    #sum of infinite geometric series
    #a1 = P(hold, break) (win) or P(get broken, don't break back) (loss)
    #r = P(hold, don't break) + P(get broken, break back)
    r = (p_point_a * p_point_b) + ((1 - p_point_a) * (1 - p_point_b))
    p_win += p_extended * (p_point_a * (1 - p_point_b) / (1 - r))
        
    return p_win

def p_set (p_point_a, p_point_b, ad_scoring, sets_to, tiebreak_at, tiebreak_to):
    p_hold = p_game(p_point_a, ad_scoring)
    p_break = p_game(1 - p_point_b, ad_scoring)
    p_set_outcomes = np.zeros((2, sets_to + 1))

    # not tiebreak (i.e., 6-6) nor extended set (i.e., 7-5)
    for n_games in range(sets_to, 2 * sets_to - 1):
        losing_score = n_games - sets_to
        p_winning_position = 0
        p_losing_position = 0

        n_service_games = math.floor(n_games / 2)
        n_return_games  = math.floor((n_games - 1) / 2)

        for paired_breaks in range(losing_score + 1):
            n_holds_winning = n_service_games - paired_breaks
            n_breaks_winning = (sets_to - 1) - n_holds_winning
            p_winning_position += (
                  stats.binom.pmf(n_holds_winning, n_service_games, p_hold)
                * stats.binom.pmf(n_breaks_winning, n_return_games, p_break))
            
            n_holds_losing = losing_score - paired_breaks
            n_breaks_losing = paired_breaks
            p_losing_position += (
                  stats.binom.pmf(n_holds_losing, n_service_games, p_hold)
                * stats.binom.pmf(n_breaks_losing, n_return_games, p_break))

        #player 1 serving in the last game of set
        if n_games % 2:
            p_set_outcomes[0, losing_score] = p_winning_position * p_hold
            p_set_outcomes[1, losing_score] = p_losing_position * (1 - p_hold)
        else:
            p_set_outcomes[0, losing_score] = p_winning_position * p_break
            p_set_outcomes[1, losing_score] = p_losing_position * (1 - p_break)

    #special ending (i.e., gets to 5-5)
    p_special = 0
    for paired_breaks in range(sets_to):
        n_service_games = sets_to - 1
        n_return_games = n_service_games
        p_special += (
                  stats.binom.pmf(n_service_games - paired_breaks, n_service_games, p_hold)
                * stats.binom.pmf(paired_breaks, n_return_games, p_break))

    if tiebreak_at:
        #extended set
        #P(hold, don't break) + P(get broken, break back)
        p_trade_games = (p_hold * (1 - p_break)) + ((1 - p_hold) * p_break)
        for i in range(tiebreak_at - (sets_to - 1)):
            p_set_outcomes[0, sets_to - 1] += (p_special 
                                               * p_trade_games ** i
                                               * (p_hold * p_break))
            p_set_outcomes[1, sets_to - 1] += (p_special
                                               * p_trade_games ** i
                                               * ((1 - p_hold) * (1 - p_break)))
        
        #tiebreak
        p_tiebreak_score = p_special * (
                                        (p_hold * (1 - p_break) + (1 - p_hold) * p_break)
                                        ) ** (tiebreak_at - (sets_to - 1))
        p_tiebreak_win = p_tiebreak(p_point_a, p_point_b, tiebreak_to)
        p_set_outcomes[0, sets_to] = p_tiebreak_score * p_tiebreak_win
        p_set_outcomes[1, sets_to] = p_tiebreak_score * (1 - p_tiebreak_win)
        
    else:
        #extended set
        #sum of infinite geometric series
        #a1 = P(hold, break) (win) or P(get broken, don't break back) (loss)
        #r = P(hold, don't break) + P(get broken, break back)
        r = (p_hold * (1 - p_break)) + ((1 - p_hold) * p_break)
        p_set_outcomes[0, sets_to - 1] = p_special * (p_hold * p_break / (1 - r))
        p_set_outcomes[1, sets_to - 1] = p_special * ((1 - p_hold) * (1 - p_break) / (1 - r))
    
    return p_set_outcomes

def p_match(
        p_point_a, p_point_b, num_sets, ad_scoring = True,
        sets_to = 6, tiebreak_at = 6, tiebreak_to = 7,
        final_tiebreak_at = False):
    
    p_win = 0
    sets_to_win = math.ceil(num_sets / 2)
    p_win_set = sum(p_set(p_point_a, p_point_b,
                      ad_scoring, sets_to,
                      tiebreak_at, tiebreak_to
                     )[0])
    
    for n_losing_sets in range(sets_to_win):
        if n_losing_sets == sets_to_win - 1:
            p_win += (stats.binom.pmf(sets_to_win - 1,
                                      n_losing_sets + sets_to_win - 1,
                                      p_win_set)
                     * sum(p_set(p_point_a, p_point_b,
                           ad_scoring, sets_to,
                           final_tiebreak_at, tiebreak_to
                          )[0]))
        else:
            p_win += (stats.binom.pmf(sets_to_win - 1,
                                      n_losing_sets + sets_to_win - 1,
                                      p_win_set)
                     * p_win_set)
        
    return p_win

The probabilities of winning 6-0, 7-5, or 7-6 are unaffected by serve order. However, the probabilities of winning 6-1, 6-2, 6-3, and 6-4 are different based on who serves first. If you serve first, winning 6-3 means you broke one more time than your opponent, but if you serve second, winning 6-3 means you broke two more times than your opponent. But the probabilty of winning 6-3 when you serve first is also not equal to the probability of winning 6-4 if you serve second, even though both scores mean you broke one more time than your opponent. That's because the number of games that your opponent served differs by one (four times when you serve first, 5 times when you serve second).

The part that surprised me is that the total probability of winning 6-1, 6-2, 6-3, and 6-4 is unaffected by serve order. It's just distributed differently among the four outcomes. My expectations were incorrect because of an availability bias: it was easy to see that the probability of winning 6-3 was affected by serve order, and I expected that to mean that the total probability of winning was also affected.

def create_format_win_rates(num_sets = 3, ad_scoring = True,
        sets_to = 6, tiebreak_at = 6, tiebreak_to = 7,
        final_tiebreak_at = False):

    num_points = 51
    
    win_rates = np.zeros((num_points, num_points))
    probabilities = np.linspace(0.25, 0.75, num_points)
    for i in range(len(probabilities)):
        for j in range(len(probabilities)):
            win_rates[i,j] = p_match(probabilities[i],
                                     probabilities[j],
                                     num_sets, ad_scoring, sets_to,
                                     tiebreak_at, tiebreak_to, final_tiebreak_at
                                    )
    return win_rates

Expected Slam Wins by Format, a Toy Example¶

The differences between win probabilities between these formats still seem fairly abstract. Just how much does a 5% increase in win probability matter?

I'm going to make this difference concrete with a toy example. Slam tournaments have a main draw of 128 players, which means the player who wins the tournament wins seven out of seven singles matches. Seven successes in seven trials might point you to a certain probability distribution: the binomial distribution, which I discussed above.

There are many reasons why the probability of winning a Slam is not described well by this probability model and the binomial distribution. Here's just one: players generally face tougher opponents in later rounds of a tournament, so the percent of service and return points won by a player should decline in later rounds. After all, if you flip a 65% coin for your serves and a 60% coin for your returns, and you meet someone who flips a 70% coin for their serves and a 65% coin for their returns, what would you do?

But just for the moment, let's pretend this model did extend to Slam tournaments, and no matter who you played, only you determined the percent of service and return points that you win. How would you do?

def plot_expected_slam_wins(
    slam_win_prob_match_probs, n_slams, player_serve_win_rate,
    opponent_serve_win_rate, match_formats,
    figsize= figsize, x_labels_every = 5, xmax=False):
    
    f, subplots = plt.subplots(figsize=figsize,
                               nrows = 2,
                               ncols = len(match_formats),
                               sharex = True,
                               sharey = 'row'
                              )
    x = np.linspace(0, n_slams, n_slams + 1)
    x_ticks = x[x%x_labels_every==0]
    cmap = plt.cm.get_cmap('plasma')
    color_space = np.linspace(0.1, 0.9, len(match_formats))
    
    subplots[0,0].set_ylabel('P(Win N Events)', size='large')
    subplots[1,0].set_ylabel('P(Win At Least N Events)', size='large')
    
    for i in range(len(match_formats)):
        slam_win_p = slam_win_prob_match_probs.loc[
                        (player_serve_win_rate, opponent_serve_win_rate),
                        [match_formats[i]]
                        ]
        color = cmap(color_space[i])
        subplots[0, i].set_title(match_formats[i])
        subplots[0, i].tick_params(labelleft=True, labelbottom=True)
        subplots[1, i].tick_params(labelleft=True, labelbottom=True)


        sns.barplot(x,
                    stats.binom(n_slams, slam_win_p).pmf(x),
                    color=color,
                    ax = subplots[0, i]
                   )
        sns.barplot(x,
                    np.insert(1 - stats.binom(n_slams, slam_win_p).cdf(x[0:len(x)-1]),
                              0, 1),
                    color=color,
                    ax = subplots[1, i]
                   )
        
    subplots[0,0].set(xticks=x_ticks, xticklabels=x_ticks)
    
    if xmax:
        subplots[0,0].set_xlim(right=xmax)
    f.tight_layout()

Serena Williams¶

Now let's look at Serena's expected Slam totals. In the last 52 weeks, she has won about 64% of service points and 44% of return points (according to Tennis Abstract). If she played just 20 Slams, how would she do?

These are overestimates primarily because competition becomes tougher as the tournament progresses, so Serena can be expected to win a lower percentage of points both when serving and returning, thus decreasing her win probability in later matches.

In a best of 5 format and this coin flipping model, the most likely number of Slam wins would be 10. In fact, there's a roughly 70% chance that Serena would win at least 10 Slams. But in a best of 3 format, her chances of winning at least 10 Slams is closer to 10%.

Serena has actually won 23 Slams in about 70 attempts.

This model of expected slam wins is very concrete, but also ridiculous. Consider the plight of Andy Murray. Throughout his career at Grand Slam tournaments, he has won about 66% of his service points and 42% of his return points (according to Tennis Abstract), but he only has 3 Championships to show for it. He's lost 8 Grand Slam finals, 3 to Federer and 5 to Novak Djokovic. 8 of his 10 losses in Grand Slam Semis have come from Federer, Nadal, and Djokovic. Any model that ignores variation in the strength of opponents is going to seriously overestimate success at the highest levels.

But even so, this analysis, including its overly simplistic final model, is helpful for establishing an intuition for how tennis formats affect favorites going into a match. We already knew that better players should prefer longer matches, but now we have an initial framework for how to think about the magnitude of the difference.
