Counting Games Between Service Breaks - The Geometric Distribution

Thu 21 February 2019
By Joshuah Kapilivsky

Part 1: The Geometric Distribution¶

Consider the following simple model of tennis:

  1. Players hold serve at a rate greater than 50%
  2. Players win sets by holding serve more often than their opponent
  3. Most sets are won by a single break
  4. Past game outcomes have no impact on future game outcomes

This model, especially ideas 3 and 4, is clearly oversimplified. But consider the last tennis match you watched, especially one between two strong servers. It probably felt like everyone was waiting to see how many games it would take for someone to get a break. In fact, the narratives pushed by the commentators probably reinforced this point of view.

Lucky for us, some basic statistics can give us an expectation for how many games we'll have to wait to see a break. I'm going to simplify the question one step further: how many of Federer's return games do we need to wait between breaks?

If Federer had a 50% chance of breaking in any return game, this would be mathematically equivalent to "how many times do I have to flip this coin before it comes up heads?" We know intuitively that sometimes we won't have to wait long at all, and other times we'll have to wait for a while. We tend to be surprised the longer the streak of tails. Let's take a look at how long we should have to wait.

def plot_geom_theoretical(p, ax=False):
    x = np.arange(geom.ppf(0.001, p),
                  geom.ppf(0.999, p))
    if ax==False:
        fig, ax = plt.subplots(1, 1)
    ax.vlines(x, 0, geom.pmf(x, p), colors='b', lw=8)
    ax.set_title('Theoretical Run Lengths with Rate of '
             + str(round(p, 2)))
    ax.set_xlabel('Run Length')
    ax.set_ylabel('Probability')
    ax.set_ylim(ymin=0)
    ax.tick_params(labelleft=True)    

    
plot_geom_theoretical(0.5)

This is known as the geometric distribution.

The geometric distribution describes the probability of observing k-1 consecutive failures, followed by success on the kth trial. Mathematically, the probability mass function is:

f(k) = {(1-p)^(k-1)}(p)

You could have probably written this down for the coin toss example without any help. The first term is the probability of k-1 failures, and the second term is the probability of success on a single trial.

Under some basic assumptions, this simple formula could hold for the number of games until the next break in tennis for a single returner. Just like with coin flips, we include the game when the break happens in the run length, so a run length of 5 means 4 consecutive holds followed by a break.

If your favorite player breaks at about 25%, this is the distribution you might expect:

But here's the question you should be asking: "well, does this hold up in real life?" I don't know, but that's what I'm going to find out.

Part 2: Break Rates and Run Lengths from Real Tennis Data¶

I'm going to turn to ATP and WTA point by point data collected by Jeff Sackmann of Tennis Abstract and Heavy Topspin. I obtained the raw data from his GitHub page, and it is licensed under a Creative Commons Attribution-NonCommercial-ShareAlike 4.0 International License.

The dataset is pretty intuitive:

  • Every match has a string of characters.
  • There is one character for the outcome of every point. S indicates the server won, R indicates the returner won. (Sometimes aces and doublefaults are indicated as A and D).
  • Games are separated by semicolons, and sets are separated by periods.

I'm going to convert these to strings of service holds and breaks and attribute them to the player returning serve. I'm then going to create break rates and run lengths from the data.

# combine ReturnGames across matches to create run lengths
def combine_across_matches(df, groupby_vars):
    returners_list = df.groupby(groupby_vars)['ReturnGames'].sum()
    returners_num_matches = (df
                            .groupby(groupby_vars)['ReturnGames']
                            .size()
                            .rename('NumMatches')
                            )
    
    tours = df.groupby(groupby_vars)['Tour'].first()
    _returners = pd.concat([returners_list, returners_num_matches, tours], axis=1)
    _returners['NumReturnGames'] = [len(x) for x in _returners['ReturnGames']]
    _returners['NumBreaks'] = [x.count('B') for x in _returners['ReturnGames']]
    _returners['BreakRate'] = _returners['NumBreaks'] / _returners['NumReturnGames']
    _returners['BreakIndices'] = [[i + 1 for i, outcome in enumerate(games) if outcome == 'B']
                                 for games in _returners['ReturnGames']]
    
    #Intentionally skip the first and last run (because those are actually of unknown length).
    _returners['BreakRunLengths'] = [[y - x for x,y in zip(l, l[1:])]
                                     for l in _returners['BreakIndices']]
    
    return _returners

Part 3: Could this Data have been Generated from the Geometric Distribution?¶

Estimation, Probability, and Likelihood¶

Let's discuss three related concepts: estimation, probability, and likelihood. Casual statisticians (read: humans) are generally familiar with the concept of probability, but likelihood as a distinct concept is probably less familiar.

Consider this basic model for working with data:

  1. Someone has data on a process.
  2. Someone creates a model of the process.
  3. Someone uses the model to make predictions for the process.

Obviously this, like any model, is incredibly simplified.

Probability is usually used to describe going from step 2 to step 3. Take flipping a coin for example: you have a model that the probability of obtaining heads is 50%. You generally don't ask about probability for existing data ("what's the probability that the coin I've already flipped and looked at is heads?") because the outcome is known (either it is or it isn't heads).

Estimation is (part of) the process of going from step 1 to step 2. Given some data, what is the best model I can create? Usually estimation refers to picking parameter values, like coming up with the 50% value for a fair coin.

Likelihood is usually used to describe the reverse. Literally it means "how plausible is it that if this model were reality I'd obtain data like this?" It's used to evaluate claims about the suitability of a model. If I flip 100 coins and they all come up tails, my model of the coin as 50% heads is not very likely. It's still possible that this is the "right" model, but a better model would have a different value than 50%.

In this context, I've started by claiming that break run lengths are geometrically distributed. We can use a single parameter value, the break rate, to model the run lengths with the geometric distribution. The question we're asking is, "Is this geometric model suited for the data we've already observed?" That's a question about likelihood.

In [11]:
def get_break_runs_p(in_df, min_breaks = 2):
    break_rate = in_df.BreakRate
    break_runs = in_df.BreakRunLengths
    num_breaks = in_df.NumBreaks
    
    if num_breaks > min_breaks:
        break_run_counts = Counter(break_runs)
        _df = pd.DataFrame.from_dict(break_run_counts, orient='index')
        _df = _df.reindex(range(1, max(break_runs)+1)).rename(columns={0: 'Observed'})
        _df.index.rename('RunLength', inplace=True)
        _df['Expected'] = (1-break_rate)**(_df.index-1) * break_rate * (num_breaks - 1)

        _df['LogLikelihood'] = _df.Observed * np.log(_df.Observed / _df.Expected)
        log_likelihood = 2 * _df.LogLikelihood.sum()
        degrees_of_freedom = _df.Observed.count() - 1
        return 1 - chi2.cdf(log_likelihood, degrees_of_freedom)
    
    else:
        return np.nan
    
returners['P-Value'] = returners.apply(get_break_runs_p, axis=1)
returner_matchups['P-Value'] = returner_matchups.apply(get_break_runs_p, axis=1)

Normally, one should be wary of performing hundreds of hypothesis tests. There's a wealth of literature on the perils of multiple hypothesis testing (I consider this the definitive source) and how to adjust significance thresholds based on the number of tests. But for our purposes this will suffice. A reasonable model would be significant only about 5% of the time, and that's clearly not the case.

Turns out the evidence for modeling this as a geometric distribution is pretty bad. But that's not totally surprising; for the geometric distribution to be relevant the probability of breaking serve must be constant. There are many factors that might cause a returner to break serve at different rates over the years. Perhaps the most important is changes in the quality of the opponent. Ideally opposition gets better as tournaments progress, so it should be harder to break serve in later matches. That means you'd likely get shorter than expected break runs early in a tournament (when opponents are not as strong) and longer than expected break runs late in a tournament (when opponents are stronger).

Break Run Lengths for Specific Returner-Server Matchups¶

Now I;m going to refine my research question: do we get geometrically distributed break runs when considering a single returner against a single server (across matches)?

I've been explicit here about returner-server matchups because servers alternate in tennis. So every match has two break runs in progress, roughly "How many of Djokovic's return games has it been since he last broke Nadal?" and "How many of Nadal's return games has it been since he last broke Djokovic?" I've separated them because tracking just one run would involve two alternating probabilities: the probability of Djokovic breaking serve, and the probability of Nadal breaking serve. It's known that these two probabilities are different, so the geometric distribution is not appropriate.

Part 4: Are Runs Between Breaks Geometrically Distributed?¶

So, is the geometric distribution a sufficient model for runs between breaks? Not really. The population I've examined has many more statistically significant differences from the geometric distribution than what one would expect due to chance. But when we look at head to head streaks, we see that a lot of the differences come out. We also know that players do not play at a constant level throughout their careers; players continually tweak their serves, adjust their return positioning, get injured, improve their fitness, lose confidence, and revert to the mean. We don't really expect the break rate in a particular matchup to be constant over many years.

However, this post serves two purposes:

  1. It hopefully familiarized you with the geometric distribution, as well as its basic assumptions
  2. It showed that, without any additional information, the geometric distribution is a reasonable starting point for thinking about whether a player getting broken twice in a row is anything more than a brief look at a statistical process

After all, while it is more likely that players hold multiple times between breaks, the single most likely run length, regardless of break rate, is a single game. If someone was broken last game and you had to predict precisely how many games you'd have to watch to see the next break, the best bet is actually 1 game.

That's even before you consider factors that make the geometric assumption too naive, like correlation between outcomes. Beyond the statistical reality above, if you just got broken and it actually reflects a dip in your level of play or self-confidence, you're probably even more likely to get broken again. This makes getting broken in the next game an even better bet.

What Might Actually Be Geometrically Distributed?¶

The geometric distribution is a useful but overly-simplistic tool for understanding runs between service breaks. Now that we have an intuition for its assumptions, is it better suited for another process in tennis?

There's a tension at play here: picking a process that is too simple means a positive result will be uninteresting, but picking a more complex process means a positive result is less likely. This tension is what ultimately led me to look at games between service breaks.

A more conservative process to examine might be consecutive first serves put in play. First serve percentages differ across the tours and players likely show variance in first serve percentages due to more than just sampling, but that is perhaps the simplest situation that is minimally affected by the opponent. Of course, when facing certain opponents, players likely experience pressure to either go for more on first serve or to get more first serves in play.

This leads me to consider at least two more complex analyses:

  1. Modeling runs with a compound distribution, where the probability of success is randomly sampled from another distribution. For example, perhaps the break rate is resampled after each consecutive break.
  2. Comparing the performance of modeling runs between double faults using a single double fault rate versus modeling it with separate first and second serve completion rates.

But all of those will have to wait for a follow-up anlaysis.
